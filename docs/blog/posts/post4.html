Understanding Complex and Hard-to-Read Sentences

Published: January 07, 2026

One of the most common obstacles to clear writing is the long, complicated sentence. The Client-Side Readability Checker highlights these sentences in real time, helping writers spot and simplify them before readers get lost.

The tool classifies sentences into two main categories: complex and very complex, often called hard-to-read. Complex sentences are marked in yellow, while very hard-to-read ones appear in red. These colors make it easy to scan text and identify problem areas quickly.

A sentence earns the complex label when it exceeds a certain length or contains many multisyllabic words. Very hard-to-read sentences push these factors even further, combining extreme length with dense vocabulary.

How the Tool Detects Complexity

Detection relies on proven heuristics. Sentence length is measured in words—typically anything over twenty-five words triggers a warning. Syllable count per word adds another layer: frequent use of longer words increases density.

The tool splits text into sentences accurately, even handling abbreviations, quotations, and decimal numbers correctly. This ensures highlights appear only where truly needed.

Why Long Sentences Cause Problems

  • Readers must hold more information in working memory
  • Ideas become buried under clauses and phrases
  • Risk of ambiguity rises with nested structures
  • Overall reading speed drops significantly

Short sentences, by contrast, deliver ideas with force. They create rhythm, improve comprehension, and keep attention sharp. Hemingway himself favored sentences averaging around fourteen words.

Complex sentences aren't always bad. Technical explanations sometimes require them. The key is balance—use longer sentences sparingly and only when they add value.

When you click a highlighted sentence, the tool explains the issue and suggests breaking it into shorter units or removing unnecessary words.

Practical Ways to Simplify

Start by finding the core message. Ask: what is the main action or idea? Build the sentence around that. Remove filler phrases like there are or it is important to note that.

Turn subordinate clauses into separate sentences. Replace commas with periods. Active voice often shortens sentences naturally.

As you edit, watch the highlights disappear in real time. Red turns to yellow, yellow to nothing—a satisfying visual reward for clearer prose.

Over time, spotting these patterns becomes second nature. The goal isn't to eliminate every long sentence but to ensure each one earns its place.

Clear sentences lead to clear thinking. By taming complexity, writers respect their readers' time and attention.

Simplify ruthlessly. Your readers will thank you.
