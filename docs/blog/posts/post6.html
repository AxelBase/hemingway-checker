Managing Adverb Density

Published: January 07, 2026

Adverbs often signal weak writing. Words like very, really, quickly, or suddenly can dilute impact when overused. The Client-Side Readability Checker tracks adverb density and highlights excess for revision.

Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. While sometimes necessary, they frequently prop up verbs that lack precision. He ran quickly becomes he sprinted—stronger and shorter.

The tool marks adverbs in purple, with denser concentrations earning stronger warnings. Click any highlight to see alternatives focused on vivid verbs.

How Density Is Calculated

The checker measures the ratio of adverbs to total words. A low percentage is ideal; higher ratios trigger summary panel alerts. Common -ly words are primary targets, along with intensifiers like very and quite.

Context matters. Not every -ly word is an adverb—lovely or friendly function as adjectives. The detection system accounts for this to reduce false positives.

Why Reduce Adverbs

  • Strong verbs carry more precise meaning
  • Adverbs often tell instead of show
  • They add unnecessary length
  • Overuse creates monotonous rhythm

Hemingway advised killing most adverbs. Modern style guides agree: choose exact verbs over modified weak ones. She whispered softly becomes she whispered—softly is redundant.

Some adverbs serve well: time markers like yesterday or frequency words like often. The tool focuses on modifiable cases where stronger alternatives exist.

Strategies for Replacement

Search for -ly words systematically. For each, ask if a better verb exists. Walked slowly becomes ambled or strolled. Said angrily becomes snapped or growled.

Replace very + adjective with a single stronger word: very tired becomes exhausted, very large becomes enormous. This tightens prose dramatically.

Use specific details to show rather than tell with adverbs. Instead of walked confidently, describe posture, stride, or expression.

As edits remove adverbs, purple highlights fade. The summary panel updates density in real time, providing immediate feedback.

Cutting adverbs sharpens voice and pace. Writing gains authority. Readers trust precise language.

Fewer adverbs force better word choices. The result is vigorous, memorable prose that stands on its own.

Mastering adverb control elevates writing from competent to compelling.

Choose strong verbs. Let adverbs rest.
